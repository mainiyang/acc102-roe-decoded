# Reflection

## 1. Why this project?

At the beginning, I honestly wasn't very sure what topic to choose.
There were a few options, but I ended up going with DuPont analysis
mainly because it felt more structured. Breaking ROE into components
made it easier to understand compared to just looking at one number.

Also, the appendix in the assignment gave some hints (like the pivot
tables), so it felt like a safer starting point. I didn't have a very
deep understanding of ROE before this --- my thinking was quite
straightforward: higher ROE = better company.

So my goal at the start was just to see if that assumption actually
holds when looking at real company data.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. One moment that genuinely surprised me

One thing that really surprised me was the comparison between Costco and
Walmart.

I originally thought Costco would have much higher profit margins
because of its membership model. But when I actually saw the numbers,
their margins were quite close, yet their ROE was quite different.

That made me pause a bit. It showed me that the difference wasn't really
about pricing power like I first assumed, but more about how efficiently
the business runs (like turnover and capital usage).

Another moment was seeing Home Depot's negative equity. At first I
thought something was wrong with the data, but later I realized it was
due to share buybacks. That was a bit eye-opening, because it made me
realize financial data can look strange but still be correct.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 3. Responding to the instructor's feedback

In the beginning, my notebook only had about four charts, and the app
had even fewer. After the instructor mentioned that there should be more
charts and better logic, I realized the issue wasn't just "not enough
charts."

It was more that the charts didn't really connect with each other.

So after that, I expanded the notebook to around ten charts and tried to
organize them in a more natural order --- starting from basic breakdown,
then changes over time, then trying to explain why those changes
happened, and finally comparing across companies.

For the app, I didn't include all the charts. I kept around six that
felt more useful for interaction. Some charts just made more sense in
the notebook because they needed explanation.

What I took from this is that adding more charts is actually the easy
part. Making them connect logically is much harder.

## 6. What I take away

Now when I see a high ROE, I don't immediately think "this company is
good."

I think more about why it is high --- is it margins, turnover, or
leverage.

Also, I realized that analysis is not just about getting results. If the
results don't connect in a clear way, it's actually hard for anyone to
understand them.
